Paraphrasing:

  • Your own rendition of essential information and ideas expressed by someone else is presented in a new form
  • Don't directly quote too much
  • Try to understand it as a whole rather than pursuing to write down ideas
  • Be selective
  • Your own opinions can be used
  • You can also use direct quotations without changing or using quotation marks
  • Record the source inclusive of page numbers
  • Write keywords to indicate subject
  • Use quotation to identify any unique term or phrase
  • Set original aside and make sure it doesn't lose its essence

Summary:

  • Ignore specifics
  • Write main points and maybe ignore minor details
  • Cover the main idea
  • Any words or phrases directly taken put in ""
  • The tone of the summary should be neutral
  • Length should be strictly followed

APA:

  • In this citation, we have the last name, initials, and the year in brackets and then the article name, then the title in sentence case(Pride and prejudice)
  • Title in italics
  • In the end Location: Publisher
  • If authors > 6, mention 1 and use et al.
  • All APA citations should use hanging indents, that is, the first line of an entry should be flush left, and the second and subsequent lines should be indented ¬Ω
  • If there is more than one author, use an ampersand (&) before the name of the last author. If there are more than six authors, list only the first one and use et al. for the rest.
  • Place the date of publication in parentheses immediately after the name of the author. Place a period after the closing parenthesis. Do not italicize, underline, or put quotes around the titles of shorter works within longer works.
  • Examples:
  • Searles, B., & Last, M. (1979). A reader's guide to science fiction. New York: Facts on File, Inc.
  • Bergmann, P. G. (1993). Relativity. In The new encyclopedia britannica (Vol. 26, pp. 501-508). Chicago: Encyclopedia Britannica.
  • Harlow, H. F. (1983). Fundamentals for preparing psychology journal articles. Journal of Comparative and Physiological Psychology, 55, 893-896.
  • Note: Do not enclose the title in quotation marks. Put a period after the title. If a periodical includes a volume number, italicize it and then give the page range (in regular type) without "pp." If the periodical does not use volume numbers, as in newspapers, use p. or pp. for page numbers.
  • Note: Unlike other periodicals, p. or pp. precedes page numbers for a newspaper reference in APA style
  • Note: When citing Internet sources, refer to the specific website document. If a document is undated, use "n.d." (for no date) immediately after the document title. Break a lengthy URL that goes to another line after a slash or before a period. Continually check your references to online documents. There is no period following a URL.
  • Note: If you cannot find some of this information, cite what is available.
  • If we have a revised edition, use the newest date in curly brackets and the old year in square brackets
  • Use Ed. and Eds. for editors in the citation and put name in reverse as in initial first and last name

  • GVU's 8th WWW user survey. (n.d.). Retrieved August 8, 2000, from http://www.cc.gatech.edu/gvu/usersurveys/survey1997-10/

Parts of a Critique:

  1. Introduction
  • Gives a brief statement of your overall evaluation of the work
  • Restates the overall view of the article highlighting strengths and weaknesses
  • Gives names of authors and titles
  • Reports aims of work and data and methods used
  1. Evaluation
  • Are you convinced by the conclusions drawn by the authors
  • How good are the evidence, data, and methods used
  • Report main findings and conclusions
  1. Conclusion
  • Can you see any limitations of research not mentioned by the authors
