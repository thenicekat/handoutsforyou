Unemployment

Measuring Unemployment

  • If a member ages 16 or more worked 1 hour or more as a paid employee he is classified as employed
  • A household member is also considered employed if he or she worked 15 hours or more without pay in a family enterprise
  • A household member is counted as employed if the person held a job from which he or she was temporarily absent because of illness
  • Those who are not employed come into either 1. unemployed or 2. not in the labor force
  • labor force = employed + unemployed
  • population = labor force + not in labor force
  • unemployment rate = employed/(employed + unemployed)
  • labor force participation rate = labor force/population

Costs of Unemployment

Frictional, Structural and Cyclical Unemployment

  • The portion of unemployment resulting from normal turnover of labor market is called Frictional Unemployment
  • Used to denote short run job/skill matching problems
  • The portion of unemployment that is as a result of changes in structure of economy is called structural unemployment and this results in significant loss of jobs in certain industries
  • This denotes longer run adjustment problems
  • The unemployment rate that occurs as a normal part of the functioning of the economy.
  • Sometimes is taken as the sum of frictional and structural unemployment rate
  • Cyclical unemployment is the unemployment that is above frictional plus structural unemployment

Inflation and Deflation

  • Consumer price index (CPI): A price index computed each month by the Bureau of Labor Statistics using a bundle that is meant to represent the “market basket” purchased monthly by the typical urban consumer.
  • Producer price indexes (PPIs): Measures of prices that producers receive for products at all stages in the production process.
  • Once called wholesale price indexes, PPIs are calculated separately for various stages in the production process.

Long Run Growth

  • Output growth: The growth rate of the output of the entire economy.
  • Per-capita output growth: The growth rate of output per person in the economy.
  • Productivity Growth: The growth rate of output per worker.
