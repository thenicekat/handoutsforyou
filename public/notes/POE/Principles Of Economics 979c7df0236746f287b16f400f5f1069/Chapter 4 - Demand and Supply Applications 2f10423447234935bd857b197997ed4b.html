Price Rationing

  • Happens when there is excess demand
  • Not everyone can be satisfied with the goods
  • Only people who pay more get most of the good
  • In situations like these you can argue that everything has a price you can but it with but this is not always true.
  • There might be an emotional attachment to an object which you wouldn’t sell out for any price

Alternate Rationing Mechanisms

  • Price Ceiling was a maximum price that sellers may charge for a good, usually set by the government
  • Price minimum is the minimum price at which you can buy something

Queuing

  • Waiting in line as a means of distributing goods and services
  • Non price rationing mechanism
  • Sacrifice was measured in hours and aggravation instead of dollars

Favored Customers

  • Many customers tried to become favored by offering side payments
  • Owners also charged high prices for service and hid the actual price using service overcharges

Ration Coupons

  • Families would be given ration tickets or coupons
  • That way everyone would get the same amount regardless of income
  • When ration coupons are used with no prohibition against trading them, the result is almost same as price rationing. Those who are willing to pay will always buy more coupons
  • If there is a prohibition against trading them, it’s impossible to stop black markets

Rationing in Concerts

  • If too expensive, fans would be furious and ordinary loyal fans won’t be able to pay for it
  • Will lead to queueing and virtual waiting rooms
  • Black Marketing
  • Favored Customers

Prices and Allocation of Resources

Price Floor

  • Price floor is the minimum price below which exchange is not permitted
  • If price floor is set above the equilibrium price, the result will be excess supply
  • Example: Minimum Wage

Supply and Demand and Market Efficiency

Consumer Surplus

  • Consumer Surplus is the difference between how much a consumer is willing to pay for the good and the market price
  • Say the good is priced at 2.5$ but a consumer is willing to pay upto 5$, this 2.5$ is the consumer surplus, basically what money consumer has
  • But this is not consistent, some have more consumer surplus than others
  • The total Blue area on the right is the consumer surplus

Producer Surplus

  • Producer Surplus is the difference between the current market price and the cost of production for the firm
  • If a good is priced at 2.5$ but he can make it in 0.75$, the difference of 1.75$ is the producer surplus

Market Equilibrium

  • The total loss of producer and consumer surplus from underproduction and over production is referred to as deadweight loss

Government Policies

  • The price of the good has been regulated at a fixed price Pmax, i.e. there is a price ceiling
  • In this case, the producer is sure to get a loss
  • Here the consumer might also face loss if B>A
  • Economic efficiency: Maximization of aggregate consumer and producer surplus
  • Market failure is a situation where an unregulated competitive market is inefficient because prices fail to provide proper signals to consumers and producers
    • Externalities: Action taken by either a producer or a consumer which affects other producers/consumers
    • Lack of information
  • Deadweight loss is the total consumer plus producer surplus
